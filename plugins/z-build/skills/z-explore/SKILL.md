# z-explore

## Overview

Explore the design space and agree on a direction *before* committing to a plan. The point is to surface real alternatives and pick deliberately — not to lock in the first idea that surfaced.

**Core principle: cheap to explore, expensive to unwind.** A wrong direction caught here costs a sentence or a throwaway mockup; caught after implementation it costs the implementation.

## When to Use

- Net-new features, open-ended asks, or anything design/UX-shaped where look-and-feel matters
- The approach genuinely isn't obvious — multiple defensible directions exist
- The user is thinking out loud ("I have an idea…", "what should this look like?")

**Skip it when the approach is already clear** — go straight to `z-plan`. Don't manufacture alternatives for a task with one obvious answer.

## How

1. **Understand the intent.** Ask one or two targeted questions about purpose, constraints, and what "good" looks like. Don't interrogate.
2. **Propose 2–3 approaches** with honest tradeoffs, lead with a recommendation and why. For conceptual choices, do this in the terminal.
3. **For visual questions, show don't tell.** When the answer is a layout, look, diagram, or "which of these," use the visual companion.
   **REQUIRED:** read [visual-companion.md](visual-companion.md) before starting the server.
4. **Converge on a direction** and state it back in a sentence or two.

Then hand off to `z-plan` to turn the direction into ordered steps. Ideation is conversation (plus optional mockups) — **no design docs, no `.planning/` files.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Locking the first idea** — surface 2–3 real options before choosing.
- **Describing a visual decision in prose** — if it's about layout or look, show it in the companion.
- **Turning ideation into a spec ceremony** — the output is an agreed direction, not a document.
- **Exploring a task that has one obvious approach** — skip straight to `z-plan`.
- **Manufacturing fake alternatives** — if there's really one good way, say so and move on.
